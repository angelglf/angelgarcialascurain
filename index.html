About

My current work studies how tariffs and rules of origin reshape North American supply chains, where foreign firms choose to locate in Mexico and what that means for regional welfare, and how labor market power interacts with informality in the Mexican economy.

Before starting my PhD, I completed an M.S. in Economics & Computation at Duke University, where I worked as a research assistant for Prof. Matthias Kehrig and Prof. Marjorie McElroy. I previously spent two years as an economist in the General Directorate of Economic Research at Banco de México, and hold a B.A. in Economics from Universidad Iberoamericana in Mexico City.

I am expected to complete my PhD in 2027.

Working Papers

Tariffs, Rules of Origin, and the Reorganization of North American Supply Chains

How do firms organize their North American supply chains under different configurations of tariffs and rules of origin? A global sourcing model with heterogeneous firms choosing sourcing sets and USMCA compliance, used to evaluate how tariffs and rules of origin jointly shape compliance rates, supply chain structure, and Mexican welfare.

Foreign Firm Footprints: Entry, Agglomeration and Welfare in Mexico

What drives foreign and domestic firm entry across Mexican labor markets? Entry patterns estimated via Poisson regression feed counterfactuals on how U.S. tariff impositions would affect firm location, input markets, and welfare across Mexican regions.

Labor Market Power and Informality (with Octavio Vera)

Documents differences in wage markdowns across the formal and informal sectors in Mexico, and proposes a theory linking labor market power to the structure of formal employment costs.
